Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology (DLT) that records transactions across multiple computers in a secure, transparent, and tamper-proof manner.
Instead of relying on a central authority (like banks or governments), blockchain allows peer-to-peer transactions using cryptographic security.

Each record (block) contains:

Transaction data

A timestamp

A cryptographic hash of the previous block

This creates an immutable chain of blocks â€” hence the term blockchain.

Transaction Initiation: A user requests a transaction (e.g., sending cryptocurrency).

Verification: The transaction is verified by nodes (computers) in the network using consensus algorithms like Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS).

Block Creation: Verified transactions are grouped into a new block.

Linking: Each block references the previous blockâ€™s hash, forming a secure chain.

Immutable Record: Once added, the data in a block cannot be altered or deleted.

ğŸ”¹ Case Study Example: Blockchain in Supply Chain Management

Problem:
Traditional supply chains face issues like lack of transparency, product fraud, and delayed tracking of goods.

Solution Using Blockchain:
Companies such as IBM and Walmart have implemented blockchain to:

Track products from origin to shelf.

Verify authenticity (e.g., food products or medicines).

Reduce fraud by providing a tamper-proof record of each step.

Result:

Enhanced transparency and trust between manufacturers, transporters, and retailers.

Faster recall of faulty products (in case of defects).

Reduction in paperwork and human error.
